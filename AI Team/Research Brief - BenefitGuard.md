# RESEARCH BRIEF

**Target Customer:** Everyday American adults with health insurance (employer-sponsored, marketplace, Medicare Advantage) who struggle to understand their coverage, fight denied claims, decode medical bills, find in-network providers, and navigate prior authorizations. Skews toward people with chronic conditions, mental health needs, or high care utilization — but the confusion is nearly universal.

**Research Date:** April 9, 2026

---

## TOP 5 PAIN POINTS

### 1. People Cannot Understand Their Own Health Insurance

**Problem statement:** The average insured American cannot correctly define basic terms like deductible, copay, coinsurance, or out-of-pocket maximum. They don't understand what their plan covers, what they'll owe, or what their EOB means. This isn't laziness — the system is built to be opaque. 51% of insured adults report difficulty understanding at least one aspect of their health insurance, and the people who *think* they understand it are often wrong.

**Verbatim quote:** "Only 4% of survey takers were able to define each term correctly, regardless of confidence level." — Policygenius found that while 83% of respondents said they "definitely understood" what a co-pay is, only 55% chose the correct definition. A 28-point gap between confidence and comprehension.

**Source:** Policygenius — https://www.policygenius.com/health-insurance/news/only-4-of-americans-understand-important-health-insurance-concepts/

**Additional source:** KFF Survey of Consumer Experiences — "About half (51%) of insured adults report some difficulty understanding at least one aspect of their health insurance, such as what their insurance will cover (36%), what they will owe out-of-pocket for care (30%), or what their explanation of benefits statement means (30%)." — https://www.kff.org/medicaid/kff-survey-shows-complexity-red-tape-denials-confusion-rivals-affordability-as-a-problem-for-insured-consumers-with-some-saying-it-caused-them-to-go-without-or-delay-care/

**Additional source:** Forbes Advisor survey found up to 77% of people were confused by basic health insurance terms including coinsurance, copayment, and deductible. — https://www.techtarget.com/healthcarepayers/news/366603974/More-than-Half-of-Americans-Have-Low-Health-Insurance-Literacy

**Urgency:** High — This confusion is the root cause of almost every other pain point. People make bad decisions because they literally do not understand the rules of the game they're forced to play.

**Willingness-to-pay signal:** 94% of insured consumers told KFF they'd support requirements on insurers to provide "simpler, easier-to-read statements explaining coverage decisions." People are begging for clarity. Policygenius, GoodRx, and other "translate the system" tools have built significant businesses on this gap. The hunger for someone to just explain this shit in plain English is real and massive.

### 2. Claims Get Denied and People Don't Know How to Fight Back

**Problem statement:** 18% of insured adults experienced a denied claim in the past year. That's roughly 1 in 5 people with private insurance. When it happens, they're blindsided. They don't know they have legal appeal rights. They don't know who to call. And the ones who try to fight it lose more often than they win. The system counts on you giving up.

**Verbatim quote:** "Most insured adults (60%) do not know they have appeal rights by law, and three quarters (76%) do not know what government agency to call for help dealing with their insurance." — KFF Survey of Consumer Experiences with Health Insurance

**Source:** KFF — https://www.kff.org/medicaid/kff-survey-shows-complexity-red-tape-denials-confusion-rivals-affordability-as-a-problem-for-insured-consumers-with-some-saying-it-caused-them-to-go-without-or-delay-care/

**Additional source:** KFF Consumer Survey on Denied Claims — "Consumers whose problems included a denied claim were half as likely to say their biggest problem was resolved satisfactorily compared to those whose problems did not include a denied claim (29% vs 59%)." And: "Even so, like insured consumers overall, most of those who experienced denied claims do not know whether they have appeal rights (69%) or what government agency to call for help (86%)." — https://www.kff.org/affordable-care-act/consumer-survey-highlights-problems-with-denied-health-insurance-claims/

**Urgency:** High — Denied claims lead to delayed care, declining health, and financial devastation. People with denied claims were twice as likely to have their health status decline and three times as likely to face unexpectedly higher out-of-pocket costs. This isn't an inconvenience. It's a health crisis.

**Willingness-to-pay signal:** Solace Health raised $74M offering human advocates to help with exactly this — appeals, paperwork, insurance fights. They charge around $200/hour. Despite mixed reviews and high prices, they've served 200K+ patients, proving demand is real even at premium price points. The r/HealthInsurance subreddit has 300K+ members, many posting specifically about how to appeal denials.

### 3. Medical Bills Are Wrong and People Pay Them Anyway

**Problem statement:** Advocacy groups report that up to 80% of medical bills contain errors — wrong codes, duplicate charges, services never rendered. (Note: this figure comes from medical billing advocates and likely overstates the rate due to selection bias. Conservative estimates put it at 30-40%. Even at the low end, that's an enormous number of incorrect bills.) Americans owe at least $220 billion in medical debt, affecting 20 million people. The bill arrives, it looks like a foreign language, and most people just pay it or let it go to collections because they don't know how to verify it, let alone dispute it. 72% of patients don't know how to appeal a billing error. 32% pay a bill immediately just to avoid the confusion, even if they suspect it's wrong.

**Verbatim quote (industry expert):** "As many as 80 percent of hospital bills contain errors. And no wonder, since there are nearly 70,000 diagnosis codes and over 71,000 procedure codes to sift through. 'To make sense of [a bill] and figure out what's right and what's wrong — it's not easy.'" — Pat Palmer, founder of Medical Billing Advocates of America, via Healthline

*Note: This quote is from a billing advocacy professional, not a consumer. Consumer-side quotes are harder to source for this pain point because most people don't realize their bill is wrong — they just pay it.*

**Source:** Healthline — https://www.healthline.com/health-news/80-percent-hospital-bills-have-errors-are-you-being-overcharged

**Additional source:** Peterson-KFF Health System Tracker — "20 million people (nearly 1 in 12 adults) owe medical debt... people in the United States owe at least $220 billion in medical debt." and "KFF polling shows that people with medical debt report cutting spending on food, clothing, and other household items, spending down their savings to pay for medical bills." — https://www.healthsystemtracker.org/brief/the-burden-of-medical-debt-in-the-united-states/

**Additional source:** Gitnux Medical Billing Statistics — "32% of patients pay a bill immediately to avoid confusion even if it might be wrong. 72% of patients do not know how to appeal a billing error. Only 0.1% of marketplace claims are appealed by patients despite high denial rates." — https://gitnux.org/medical-billing-errors-statistics/

**Urgency:** High — Medical debt is the #1 cause of bankruptcy in America. People are going broke paying bills that are literally wrong. And the system makes it nearly impossible for a normal person to figure that out.

**Willingness-to-pay signal:** Goodbill, Resolve Medical Bills, and CoPatient have all built businesses around negotiating and reviewing medical bills. Resolve reports reducing bills by thousands to zero in many cases. CoPatient found that 62% of Americans have received a bill that was more than expected. People will absolutely pay someone to fight their bills — they just need the tool to be accessible and affordable.

### 4. People Can't Figure Out Who's In-Network (And Get Burned When They're Wrong)

**Problem statement:** 23% of insured adults find it difficult to figure out which doctors, hospitals, and providers are in their plan's network. Insurer directories are notoriously inaccurate. People call their insurance, get told a doctor is in-network, show up, get treated, and then get a bill because the provider was actually out-of-network. Despite the No Surprises Act, surprise billing persists because of enforcement gaps, ground ambulance exclusions, and consent loopholes.

**Verbatim quote:** "People report an obstacle course of claims denials, limited in-network providers, and a labyrinth of red tape, with many saying it prevented them from getting needed care." — Drew Altman, President and CEO of KFF

**Source:** KFF — https://www.kff.org/medicaid/kff-survey-shows-complexity-red-tape-denials-confusion-rivals-affordability-as-a-problem-for-insured-consumers-with-some-saying-it-caused-them-to-go-without-or-delay-care/

**Additional source:** Medical Bill Rescue — "Despite federal protections that have been in place since 2022, surprise medical bills continue to plague American patients. Recent legislative efforts suggest the No Surprises Act isn't working as intended, with senators introducing new enforcement legislation in July 2025 to strengthen existing protections." — https://medicalbillrescue.com/resources/surprise-medical-bills-no-surprises-act

**Additional source:** KFF found that 91% of consumers support requirements on insurers "to maintain accurate and up-to-date information about who is in their network." — same KFF survey link above.

**Urgency:** High — One out-of-network visit can cost thousands. The No Surprises Act helped but has significant gaps. Consumers are still getting blindsided, and the insurer directories they're supposed to rely on are unreliable.

**Willingness-to-pay signal:** The CMS Transparency in Coverage rule (2022) forced insurers to publish machine-readable in-network rate files — creating a massive new data source that startups like Turquoise Health have raised $30M+ to organize. The demand for "is this doctor actually in my network?" is so strong the federal government mandated data transparency to try to solve it.

### 5. Prior Authorization Blocks and Delays Care People Need

**Problem statement:** 51% of insured adults were required to get a prior authorization in the past two years. Nearly half (47%) of those found the process difficult to navigate. 73% of all Americans say delays and denials of care by insurance companies are a "major problem." Doctors report spending an average of nearly 40 prior authorizations per week. The process delays treatment, sometimes for weeks, and patients have no idea how to push it through or what to do when it's denied.

**Verbatim quote:** "Almost half (47%) of those who were required to get a prior authorization in the past two years say it was 'somewhat difficult' (34%) or 'very difficult' (13%) to navigate the process of getting prior approval for a health care service, treatment, or needed medication." — KFF Health Tracking Poll, 2025

**Source:** KFF — https://www.kff.org/patient-consumer-protections/kff-health-tracking-poll-public-finds-prior-authorization-process-difficult-to-manage/

**Additional source:** AMA Survey — "Nearly 95% of physicians report care delays associated with prior authorization. 80% say prior authorization can lead to treatment abandonment." — https://www.ama-assn.org/press-center/ama-press-releases/ama-survey-indicates-prior-authorization-wreaks-havoc-patient-care

**Urgency:** High — This directly blocks care. People waiting weeks for approval to get an MRI, a medication, a procedure. And when it's denied, most have no idea they can appeal or how to do it. 73% of the public — across party lines — says this is a major problem.

**Willingness-to-pay signal:** The insurance industry itself pledged (in 2025, alongside HHS) to voluntarily reduce prior auth burden — a signal that even insurers recognize this is a crisis. But 60% of the public doesn't think they'll follow through. There's a vacuum for a consumer-side tool that helps people navigate, expedite, and appeal prior auth decisions.

## RED FLAGS

**1. Consumer willingness to pay for D2C health tools is unproven at scale.** Redesign Health explicitly calls this out: "Willingness to pay for a novel D2C healthcare platform is always a risk given patient preference for paying for healthcare through their payer." HSA/FSA payment is a potential mitigation, but it's untested territory. — Source: https://redesignhealth-insights.webflow.io/insights/d2c-care-navigation

**2. Trust barrier.** People are skeptical of AI giving health-adjacent advice. 90% of adults already struggle to understand medical information — trusting an app to interpret it for them requires earning credibility fast. Regulatory risk exists if the tool crosses from "benefits navigation" into "medical advice" territory (SaMD regulations). — Source: Redesign Health article

**3. Incumbent response.** If BenefitGuard gains traction, large insurers (UnitedHealth/Naviguard, Aetna, Cigna) could build similar features into their own apps. Oscar already does this for its own members. The risk is being "good enough"-ed by an incumbent with 50M existing users. — Source: https://www.uhc.com/member-resources/wellness-rewards-programs/naviguard-out-of-network-billing-support

**4. Data access dependency.** The CMS Transparency in Coverage files are public but massive and messy (1.7GB+ per file for Aetna alone). Maintaining a multi-insurer data pipeline is an engineering and cost challenge. Insurer cooperation is not guaranteed — they're not incentivized to make it easy for third parties.

**5. Prior failure signals.** Several startups have attempted consumer health insurance navigation tools and pivoted to B2B (employer-sold) because consumer acquisition costs in healthcare are notoriously high. The path from "people hate their insurance" to "people pay $X/month for an app" has killed companies before. The B2B "benefits navigation platform" market (Accolade, Rightway, Transcarent) exists precisely because selling to employers is easier than selling to consumers.

**6. Regulatory complexity.** Health insurance is regulated at the state level. 50 states means 50 different rules, complaint processes, consumer protection laws, and appeal procedures. Building a truly comprehensive tool requires ongoing legal research across all jurisdictions.

## RECOMMENDED IDEA

**BenefitGuard as an AI-powered, direct-to-consumer health insurance navigator.** The pain is massive, proven, and universal — 58% of insured Americans hit a problem with their coverage every single year, and only 4% actually understand how their insurance works. The existing solutions are either too expensive (Solace at $200/hr), employer-gated (Accolade, Health Advocate), or too narrow (Goodbill for bills only, Oscar for Oscar members only). Nobody is building an affordable, AI-first, comprehensive tool that helps regular people understand their benefits, fight denials, verify network status, decode bills, and navigate prior auth — all in one place. The biggest risk isn't demand. The data screams demand. The risk is monetization — can you convert this universal pain into consistent consumer revenue? That's the question the Strategist needs to pressure-test next.
